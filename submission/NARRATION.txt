Okay, this is CueChaos. It's a pass-the-phone deduction game where your friends are trying to solve the same case. Well... almost everyone is.

For this demo, we're playing Reply All. Somebody falsified a board forecast, the vote is happening at five, and two hundred forty jobs are tied to the result. So yes, the office drama got out of hand pretty quickly.

When the phone comes to you, you tap once to see your role. Most players join the investigation. One person gets the Inside Saboteur card, a motive, and three bad ideas that sound just reasonable enough to pitch to the group.

Then the team gets its first problem. The numbers changed at two thirteen in the morning. We can audit the logs, question the finance team, or restore the whole server. Nobody gets an obviously stupid option. You have to argue for a plan, hand the phone around, and vote in private.

Our group chooses the server restore. It sounds fast. It even brings the old forecast back. The catch is that it overwrites the access log, so we recover the file and lose the best way to identify who changed it.

That mistake stays on the table. Round two begins with the missing log already baked into the case, and the new evidence sits right above the next decision. You can actually point to what happened and ask, "Who kept pushing us toward the shortcut?"

At the end, we accuse Maya correctly... and still lose. We let two compromised plans through. Catching the Saboteur is only half the job; the team also has to protect the case.

I built the project in Codex with GPT-five-point-six. I used the model like a story room, not a live game master. Every case needed one goal, a deadline, a believable motive, measurable consequences, and clues that still mattered in the next round. If I could swap two scenes without breaking the story, the case went back for another pass.

Once the cases held together, Codex turned them into typed data, built the bilingual React game, and added tests around the rules. The reviewed cases ship with the site. There is no model call while somebody is playing, no API key, and no way for a visitor to spend the creator's credits.

That's CueChaos. The setting can be ridiculous. The evidence can't be.
